## Definition

The hyperbolic tangent activation function is defined as:

$$f(x) = \tanh(x) = \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{e^x + e^{-x}}$$

Alternative form: $$\tanh(x) = \frac{e^{2x} - 1}{e^{2x} + 1}$$

Relationship with sigmoid: $$\tanh(x) = 2\sigma(2x) - 1$$

where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function.

## Tanh Function and Its Derivative

![[Pasted image 20260212011132.png]]

**Key Properties:**

- Output range: **-1 to +1**
- Height is around **1**
- Zero-centered (passes through origin)
- S-shaped curve similar to sigmoid but shifted

**Derivative:** $$\tanh'(x) = 1 - \tanh^2(x)$$

Also written as: $$\frac{d}{dx}\tanh(x) = \text{sech}^2(x) = 1 - \tanh^2(x)$$

**Maximum gradient:** The derivative peaks at **1** when $x = 0$ (compared to sigmoid's 0.25)

## Advantages of Tanh

### 1. **Non-Linear**

- Non-linear function and hence can capture non-linearity
- Enables learning of complex patterns and decision boundaries
- Essential for multi-layer networks to be more powerful than single-layer models

### 2. **Differentiable**

- Differentiable everywhere on the real line
- Smooth gradient enables stable backpropagation
- Simple closed-form derivative: $\tanh'(x) = 1 - \tanh^2(x)$
- Facilitates gradient-based optimization algorithms

### 3. **Zero-Centered**

- Output range is **[-1, 1]** with mean close to zero
- The gradients calculated are both positive and negative
- This is the most significant advantage over sigmoid

**Why zero-centered matters:**

During backpropagation: $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_i} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial y} \cdot \tanh'(z) \cdot x_i$$

Since tanh outputs can be **negative, zero, or positive**, the inputs $x_i$ to subsequent layers have mixed signs. This means:

- Weights can update **independently** in different directions
- Some weights increase, others decrease in the same iteration
- Optimization follows a more **direct path** to the minimum
- **Faster convergence** compared to sigmoid

**Contrast with sigmoid:**

- Sigmoid: all outputs positive → all weights forced to move together → zig-zag path
- Tanh: outputs can be negative → weights move independently → direct path

## Disadvantages of Tanh

### 1. **Saturating Function (Vanishing Gradient Problem)**

Tanh is a **saturating function** - it squishes the input from $-\infty$ to $+\infty$ into the output range `[-1, 1]`. This makes it **prone to the vanishing gradient problem**.

**Why this happens:**

The derivative is: $$\tanh'(x) = 1 - \tanh^2(x)$$

**Key observations:**

- When $x = 0$: $\tanh'(0) = 1$ (maximum gradient)
- When $|x| > 3$: $\tanh(x) \approx \pm 1$, so $\tanh'(x) \approx 1 - 1 = 0$
- Gradients become very small for large $|x|$ values

**During deep network training:**

In backpropagation through $n$ layers: $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_1} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial a_n} \cdot \prod_{i=1}^{n-1} \tanh'(z_i)$$

**The problem:**

- Each layer multiplies the gradient by $\tanh'(z_i)$, which can be close to 0
- For saturated neurons: $\tanh'(x) \approx 0$
- Gradient diminishes exponentially as it propagates backward
- Example: $(0.1)^{10} = 10^{-10}$ - gradient vanishes!
- Early layers receive near-zero gradients
- Weight updates become negligibly small
- Network stops learning effectively

**When saturation occurs:**

- Large positive inputs: $\tanh(x) \rightarrow +1$, $\tanh'(x) \rightarrow 0$
- Large negative inputs: $\tanh(x) \rightarrow -1$, $\tanh'(x) \rightarrow 0$
- Both cases kill the gradient flow

**Impact:**

- Deep networks (>5-10 layers) struggle to train
- Lower layers learn much slower than upper layers
- May require careful weight initialization (Xavier/Glorot initialization)
- Batch normalization can help but doesn't fully solve the problem

---

### 2. **Computationally Expensive**

The formula for tanh is: $$\tanh(x) = \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{e^x + e^{-x}}$$

**Computational requirements:**

- Two exponential operations: $e^x$ and $e^{-x}$
- Subtraction and addition
- One division operation
- All operations involve floating-point arithmetic

**Cost comparison per neuron:**

- **Tanh:** 2 exponentials + 1 subtraction + 1 addition + 1 division
- **ReLU:** 1 comparison + 1 selection (max operation)
- **Sigmoid:** 1 exponential + 1 addition + 1 division

**In practical neural networks:**

- Millions of neurons across multiple layers
- Forward pass: compute tanh for every neuron
- Backward pass: compute tanh derivative for every neuron
- Training: millions of iterations
- Total: billions of exponential computations

**Consequences:**

- Slower training time compared to ReLU-based networks
- Higher energy consumption (important for mobile/edge devices)
- Memory bandwidth usage for loading exponential lookup tables
- Can be a bottleneck in real-time applications

**Modern perspective:**

- Hardware accelerators (GPUs, TPUs) optimize exponential operations
- Still significantly slower than ReLU
- Speed difference matters more in very large networks (billions of parameters)

## Comparison: Tanh vs Sigmoid

**The only advantage of tanh over sigmoid is that it is zero-centered.**

|Feature|Sigmoid|Tanh|
|---|---|---|
|**Formula**|$\frac{1}{1 + e^{-x}}$|$\frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{e^x + e^{-x}}$|
|**Range**|$(0, 1)$|$(-1, 1)$|
|**Zero-centered**|No|Yes|
|**Max Gradient**|0.25|1.0|
|**Vanishing Gradient**|Severe|Severe|
|**Computation**|1 exponential|2 exponentials|
|**Convergence Speed**|Slower|Faster (due to zero-centering)|
|**Hidden Layers**|Not recommended|Better than sigmoid, but ReLU preferred|
|**Output Layer**|Binary classification|Not typically used|

**Key insight:** While tanh is better than sigmoid for hidden layers due to zero-centering, both suffer from vanishing gradients. Modern deep networks prefer ReLU variants.

---

## When to Use Tanh

**Recommended use cases:**

- Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), LSTMs, GRUs (cell state updates)
- Shallow networks (2-3 hidden layers)
- When zero-centered outputs are critical
- Regression problems with outputs in [-1, 1] range

**Not recommended:**

- Deep feedforward networks (use ReLU instead)
- Very large-scale networks (computational cost)
- When training speed is critical
- Image classification tasks (ReLU variants work better)

## Historical Context

Tanh was widely used in neural networks during the 1990s and early 2000s as the preferred activation function over sigmoid due to its zero-centered property. However, with the rise of deep learning and very deep networks (AlexNet, VGGNet, ResNet), the vanishing gradient problem became critical, leading to the adoption of ReLU and its variants as the standard for hidden layers.

**Evolution:**

- 1990s-2000s: Sigmoid and Tan(h) dominated
- 2012: AlexNet popularized ReLU
- 2015+: ReLU variants (Leaky ReLU, ELU, SELU) became standard
- Current: Tanh still used in specific architectures (RNNs, LSTMs) but not general-purpose hidden layers
